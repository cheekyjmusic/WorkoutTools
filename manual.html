Workout Manual

Advanced Training Guide

Hypertrophy

▶

Hypertrophy is the process of increasing muscle fiber size through mechanical tension and metabolic stress. Research shows optimal growth occurs with moderate loads (65-85% 1RM) and moderate rep ranges (6-15 reps).

  • Volume matters: Aim for 10-20 sets per muscle group weekly (Schoenfeld, 2016). Total tonnage (weight × reps × sets) is a primary driver of hypertrophy.
  • Compound + isolation: Squats, bench press, rows for recruitment; leg curls, chest flyes for specific tension.
  • Time under tension: 40-60 seconds per set optimal. Slower eccentric (2-3s) increases mechanical damage.
  • Frequency: Train each muscle 2x/week minimum for superior hypertrophy (Schoenfeld et al., 2015).
  • Progressive overload: Increase weight 2.5-5% when hitting target reps, or add 1-2 reps weekly.

Nutrition: Caloric surplus of 300-500 kcal/day. Protein: 1.6-2.2g/kg body weight. Carbs support high-volume training performance.

Strength

▶

Strength training maximizes force production through neural adaptations and muscle fiber recruitment. Heavy loads (85-95% 1RM) with low reps (1-6) are most effective.

  • Intensity over volume: Heavy loads (>85% 1RM) recruit fast-twitch fibers most effectively. Even 3-5 total reps can drive strength gains.
  • Recovery emphasis: Rest 3-5 minutes between sets to recover phosphocreatine stores and CNS function (Kraemer et al., 2002).
  • Compound lifts dominate: Squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press, rows. These transfer best to real-world strength.
  • Frequency consideration: 2-3x/week per lift allows adequate CNS recovery while permitting sufficient practice volume.
  • Periodization critical: Cycle intensity waves (Heavy → Moderate → Light) every 3-4 weeks to prevent plateaus and deload.

Nutrition: Maintenance or slight surplus. Protein: 1.6-2.2g/kg. Creatine (5g/day) shown to improve strength gains by ~10% (Kreider et al., 2017).

Endurance

▶

Muscular endurance is the ability to sustain muscular force or power over extended periods. This requires high reps (12-30+) and metabolic conditioning.

  • Rep ranges: 12-20+ reps build capillarization and mitochondrial density (Schoenfeld, 2010). Higher reps increase metabolic byproduct accumulation (lactate, H+).
  • Short rest periods: 30-60 seconds maintains metabolic stress and hormonal response. Limits recovery between sets.
  • Circuit training: Alternating muscle groups allows CNS recovery while maintaining elevated heart rate. Improves work capacity.
  • Aerobic base: 2-3x/week low-intensity steady-state cardio improves oxygen utilization and recovery capacity.
  • Work capacity: Building GPP (General Physical Preparedness) through circuits and conditioning improves all athletic qualities.

Nutrition: Caloric balance maintained. Carbs critical for glycogen availability in high-rep work. Hydration essential for sweat loss.

Weight Loss

▶

Fat loss requires a caloric deficit while preserving lean muscle mass. Strength training and adequate protein are essential safeguards.

  • Caloric deficit: 300-500 kcal/day deficit yields ~0.5-1 lb/week loss. Avoid >20% deficit (increases muscle loss).
  • Preserve muscle: Strength training 3x/week with heavy loads (>75% 1RM) is most effective at preserving lean mass during deficit (Helms et al., 2014).
  • Protein critical: 2.0-2.4g/kg during deficit. High protein reduces DOMS, preserves strength, increases satiety.
  • Cardio strategic: 2-3x/week moderate-intensity preferred over excessive low-intensity. HIIT 1x/week can preserve muscle better than steady cardio.
  • Track composition: Scale weight unreliable (water/glycogen fluctuations). Use photos, measurements, how clothes fit, and strength metrics.

Nutrition: Protein: 2g/kg body weight. Prioritize whole foods for satiety. Caloric tracking initially to learn portions.

Weight Gain / Bulking

▶

Lean bulking (body recomposition) requires a controlled caloric surplus with progressive overload. The goal is muscle gain while minimizing excess fat.

  • Caloric surplus: 300-500 kcal/day surplus. Excessive surplus (>500 kcal) increases fat gain without additional muscle benefit (Helms et al., 2014).
  • Progressive overload: Primary driver of hypertrophy. Increase weight, reps, or sets consistently over weeks/months. Track lifts in a log.
  • Volume programming: 15-25 sets per muscle group weekly optimal. Split volume across 2-3 sessions per muscle.
  • Nutrient timing: Post-workout nutrition (protein + carbs within 2 hours) supports recovery and protein synthesis. Leucine threshold ~2.5g per meal.
  • Patience required: Expect 0.5-1 lb/week gain. Slower gains with better muscle/fat ratio than aggressive bulks.

Nutrition: Protein: 1.6-2.2g/kg. Carbs: 3-5g/kg (fuel training). Fats: 0.8-1.2g/kg. Micronutrient variety critical for hormonal health.

Periodization

▶

Periodization is strategic cycling of training variables to optimize adaptation, prevent plateaus, and reduce overtraining risk. Essential for long-term progression.

  • Linear periodization: Gradually increase intensity, decrease volume weekly over 4-6 weeks. Example: Week 1 (3×12), Week 2 (3×10), Week 3 (3×8), Week 4 (3×6). Proven effective for strength.
  • Undulating (daily): Vary intensity/volume within same week. Example: Monday (heavy 6 reps), Wednesday (moderate 10 reps), Friday (light 15 reps). Superior for maintaining size during strength phases.
  • Block periodization: Training blocks lasting 3-4 weeks focusing on specific qualities: Hypertrophy block → Strength block → Power block.
  • Deload importance: Every 4-8 weeks, reduce volume by 40-60% for 1 week. Allows recovery, CNS restoration, and connective tissue adaptation.
  • Conjugate method: Concurrent training of all qualities (max effort, dynamic effort, hypertrophy) within same week. Complex but highly effective for advanced lifters.

Key principle: Systematic variation prevents adaptive resistance. Beginners can progress linearly 6-12 months; advanced lifters need periodization.

Progressive Overload

▶

Progressive overload is the fundamental principle of gradually increasing training demands. Without it, adaptation plateaus within 2-3 weeks.

  • Weight progression: Increase load 2.5-5% when hitting rep target. For example: Hit 10 reps → increase weight → aim for 8-10 next week.
  • Rep progression: Add 1-2 reps weekly before increasing weight. If targeting 8-10 reps at 200 lbs, add 1 rep weekly until 10 reps achieved.
  • Volume increase: Add 1 set per exercise every 2-3 weeks. Progressive volume prevents plateau while managing fatigue.
  • Density: Complete same volume in less time. Example: Reduce rest periods from 90s to 75s, or perform 20 reps in 10 minutes instead of 12.
  • Exercise variation: Switch exercises (barbell squat → paused squat → pause-rep squats) to stress muscles differently while progressing.
  • Tracking critical: Log all sets, reps, weights. Data-driven training beats "feeling it." Review weekly for trends.

Practical example: Bench press: Week 1 (185×8,8,8) → Week 2 (185×9,9,8) → Week 3 (185×10,10,9) → Week 4 (190×8,8,8). Repeat cycle.

Nutrition for Performance

▶

Nutrition directly impacts performance, recovery, and body composition. Optimal fueling is as important as the training stimulus itself.

  • Macronutrient balance: Protein 1.6-2.2g/kg (preserves muscle), Carbs 3-7g/kg (depends on volume), Fats 0.8-1.2g/kg (hormonal health, brain function).
  • Pre-workout (1-3 hours): Carbs (40-80g) + protein (20-40g) + low fat. Examples: oats with banana, rice with chicken, pasta with lean meat. Timing optimizes glycogen and amino acid availability.
  • Intra-workout (>60 min sessions): Simple carbs (30-60g/hour) + electrolytes if training >90 minutes. Maintains blood glucose and reduces fatigue.
  • Post-workout (within 2 hours): Protein (30-50g) + carbs (80-120g). Leucine threshold ~2.5g per meal triggers mTOR. Glycogen repletion critical for recovery.
  • Daily hydration: 0.5-1 oz per lb body weight minimum. Dehydration >2% impairs strength by ~5% (Sawka et al., 2007). Electrolytes (sodium/potassium) matter for absorption and performance.

Micronutrients: Iron (oxygen transport), zinc (testosterone), magnesium (muscle function), vitamins B & D (energy, immune function). Whole food variety > supplementation for most.

Recovery & Sleep

▶

Recovery is where adaptation happens. Sleep, nutrition, and stress management are non-negotiable for progress. Overtraining without adequate recovery blocks gains.

  • Sleep duration: 7-9 hours nightly. <7 hours reduces testosterone (~20%), increases cortisol, impairs protein synthesis (Walker, 2017). Even one night of poor sleep reduces strength ~5-10%.
  • Sleep quality: Consistent bedtime, dark/cool room (65-68°F optimal), avoid screens 1 hour before bed. REM and deep sleep both critical; light exposure affects circadian rhythm.
  • Deload weeks: Every 4-8 weeks reduce volume by 40-60% for 7 days. Prevents accumulation of CNS fatigue, allows connective tissue adaptation, restores motivation.
  • Active recovery: Light cardio, stretching, yoga on off-days improve blood flow and recovery without additional stress. 20-30 min low intensity optimal.
  • Mobility work: 10-15 min daily foam rolling and stretching reduces DOMS, improves range of motion, and prevents injury. Focus on tight areas (hips, shoulders, thoracic spine).
  • Stress management: Cortisol (stress hormone) impairs recovery and promotes catabolism. Meditation, walks, social connection all reduce stress.

Signs of overtraining: Stalled strength, elevated resting HR, poor sleep, increased injury risk, mood deterioration. Solution: deload or reduce volume for 1 week.

Supplement Guide

▶

Supplements amplify good training and nutrition, not replace them. Only evidence-based supplements are worth money. Hierarchy: training → nutrition → sleep → supplementation.

  • Protein powder: Whey, casein, or plant-based. Convenient way to hit 1.6-2.2g/kg target. Whey isolate 20-30g per serving. Cost-effective vs whole food.
  • Creatine monohydrate: 5g/day, any time. Increases muscle creatine stores, improving ATP availability. ~10% strength gain, especially in strength/power traits (Kreider et al., 2017). Side effect: slight water retention (1-2 lbs).
  • Caffeine: 3-5mg/kg 30-60 min pre-workout. Increases alertness, strength output (~3%), endurance (~2-3%). Don't use daily; tolerance develops. Avoid after 2pm.
  • Beta-alanine: 3-5g/day divided doses. Increases carnosine, buffering lactate in high-rep (>60 sec) work. Modest 2-3% endurance gain. Causes harmless tingling sensation.
  • Multivitamin: Only necessary if diet lacks variety. Specific deficiencies (iron, B12, D3) matter; random megadosing doesn't help.
  • Citrulline malate: 6-8g pre-workout improves pump and reduces DOMS. Research shows ~2-3% performance improvement.

Avoid: Proprietary blends, testosterone boosters (ineffective), pre-workout "formulas" with excessive stimulants. Stick to proven standalones.

Mindset & Consistency

▶

Mental discipline and consistency determine long-term success more than any single workout. Progress is a product of small choices repeated thousands of times.

  • Goal setting: SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound). Examples: "Bench press 225 lbs in 12 weeks" vs vague "get stronger." Measurable goals drive accountability.
  • Tracking & data: Log every workout. Track lifts, body weight, photos. Data removes emotion. Weekly review identifies trends and required adjustments. Spreadsheets or apps (HEVY, Strong, JeFit) make this easy.
  • Consistency over perfection: 80% effort 90% of the time beats 100% effort 20% of the time. Missing one workout doesn't matter; missing patterns does. Build identity: "I am someone who lifts 3x/week."
  • Expected plateaus: Linear progress is impossible indefinitely. Every 3-4 weeks expect progress to stall. This is normal; adjust stimulus (increase volume, change exercises, add intensity technique).
  • Process over outcome: Control effort, consistency, technique. Don't obsess over scale weight (influenced by water, glycogen, digestion). Focus on strength metrics and composition markers.
  • Community & accountability: Training partners increase adherence 95% (vs 76% solo). Share goals with someone. Join online communities. Competition drives consistency.

Mindset shift: Reframe failure. Missed set? Learning opportunity. Bad week? Chance to reset. This growth mindset (Dweck) predicts long-term success over fixed mindset.

Hypertrophy

▶

Hypertrophy is the process of increasing muscle size through progressive resistance training. Focus on moderate to high volume, moderate reps (6–15), and short rest periods (30–90s).

  • Use compound movements (bench press, squat, deadlift) for large muscle recruitment.
  • Include isolation exercises for targeting specific muscles.
  • Prioritize form and controlled tempo for maximum tension.
  • Progressive overload is key: increase weight, reps, or sets over time.

Nutrition: Ensure a slight caloric surplus with sufficient protein (1.6–2.2g/kg).

Strength

▶

Strength training focuses on increasing the maximum force your muscles can produce. Lower reps (1–6), heavier loads, and longer rest periods (2–5 min) are typical.

  • Incorporate compound lifts like squats, bench press, and deadlifts.
  • Use periodization techniques to manage intensity and volume.
  • Accessory work targets weak points.

Nutrition: Caloric maintenance or slight surplus, focus on protein to support recovery.

Endurance

▶

Endurance training improves muscular and cardiovascular stamina. Higher reps (12–30+), lower loads, and short rest periods (30–60s).

  • Include circuits, bodyweight, and aerobic conditioning.
  • Focus on pacing and consistent technique.

Nutrition: Maintain caloric balance, prioritize carbs for energy.

Weight Loss

▶

Fat loss requires a caloric deficit while maintaining muscle mass.

  • Strength training helps preserve lean mass.
  • Cardio can be used strategically for extra calories burned.
  • Focus on high protein intake (2g/kg) and fiber-rich foods.
  • Track progress consistently (body composition, not just weight).

Weight Gain / Bulking

▶

Building lean muscle mass requires a controlled caloric surplus and progressive overload.

  • Train with moderate-high volume and compound lifts.
  • Include accessory work for symmetry and weak points.
  • Focus on nutrient-dense foods, maintain protein intake (1.6–2.2g/kg).

Periodization

▶

Periodization structures training to optimize adaptation and avoid plateaus.

  • Linear: Gradually increasing intensity over time.
  • Undulating: Frequent changes in reps and intensity.
  • Block: Focused training blocks for specific qualities.
  • Conjugate: Mix of max effort, dynamic effort, and hypertrophy work.

Progressive Overload

▶

Constantly challenging your muscles is critical for growth and strength.

  • Increase weight, reps, sets, or time under tension progressively.
  • Track lifts and adapt intelligently to avoid injury.

Nutrition for Performance

▶

Fueling your body correctly enhances performance and recovery.

  • Balance macros: protein, carbs, and fats according to goals.
  • Pre-workout: carbs + moderate protein for energy.
  • Post-workout: protein + carbs for recovery.
  • Hydration and micronutrients matter.

Recovery & Sleep

▶

Recovery is where progress happens—don't neglect it.

  • Sleep 7–9 hours per night for hormone regulation.
  • Deload every 4–8 weeks to avoid overtraining.
  • Mobility, stretching, and foam rolling help reduce injury risk.

Supplement Guide

▶

Use supplements strategically; they are not a replacement for nutrition.

  • Protein powders to meet daily intake.
  • Creatine monohydrate for strength and hypertrophy.
  • Caffeine pre-workout for focus and energy.
  • Multivitamins if your diet lacks variety.

Mindset & Consistency

▶

Training isn't just physical; mental habits matter most.

  • Set clear goals and track them.
  • Stay consistent; progress compounds over time.
  • Embrace failure as learning, not discouragement.
